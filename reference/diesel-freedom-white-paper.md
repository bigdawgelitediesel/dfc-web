## Executive Summary

The Trump Administration rescinded the 2009 Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding in February 2026 and repealed the DEF sensor mandate in March 2026, generating billions in annual savings. However, core aftertreatment systems (EGR, DPF, SCR and DEF) remain federally mandated.

The coalition argues that technology-specific hardware requirements create a false choice between clean air and operable vehicles. Current systems result in 20 to 25 percent lower fuel efficiency, 20 percent reduced power, and engine life declining from 500,000 to 1,000,000-plus miles down to approximately 350,000 miles.

The proposed solution: eliminate hardware mandates and replace them with clear, health and data-based performance standards for pollutants, allowing market-driven solutions.

## Defining the Problem

Focusing solely on tailpipe emissions ignores broader impacts. Well-to-wheel analysis shows post-2007 engines produce at least 17 percent more total pollutants over their lifetime than pre-2007 designs.

Specific concerns:
- EGR systems increase pumping losses and soot
- DPFs create backpressure and increased fuel consumption
- SCR and DEF systems add fuel penalties and sensor reliability issues
- Full deletes routinely deliver 10 to 20 percent gains in horsepower and torque

Reliability challenges cited include DPF failures, DEF crystallization, and EGR cooler malfunctions, resulting in repairs exceeding $10,000 and extended downtime. A fleet example: monthly emissions-related repair costs of $27,000 for 500 trucks.

National security: China controls 60 to 70 percent of global mining of rare earth materials needed for emissions systems.

---

## Net Environmental Benefit: Well-to-Wheel Reality

Measured correctly, reforms deliver pollution reductions. 20 to 25 percent better fuel economy means dramatically less fuel burned over the engine's life, resulting in lower CO2 and greenhouse gas emissions.

---

## The Solution: Performance-Based Standards

Abandon technology-specific hardware mandates entirely in favor of performance standards for NOx, PM, and other pollutants.

Suggested targets reference the pre-2007 performance-based approach with targets around 2.4 g/bhp-hr NMHC+NOx and 0.10 g/bhp-hr PM.

---

## Implementation Strategy and EPA Actions

1. **National Enforcement Discretion Guidance** (within 30 days). Direct field offices to exercise discretion on emissions system tampering for existing diesel engines during transition.

2. **Interim Performance-Based Standards** (within 90 days). Set clear limits measured via PEMS in real-world operation.

3. **Joint Industry-Government Working Group** (6 months). Include mechanics, fleet operators, and manufacturers to finalize targets.

4. **Codify Delete Kits and Tunes.** Approve modifications meeting performance standards.

5. **Aftertreatment Relief for Small Businesses.** Fast-track relief identified on SBA's reform list, $195 to $200 billion annual savings.

6. **Mandate Well-to-Wheel Reporting.** Require EPA to publish comprehensive environmental data.

---

## Conclusion

Eliminating mandates and adopting performance-based standards will restore efficiency, power, and durability to America's diesel engines while generating trillions in direct savings and achieving a net decrease in pollution under well-to-wheel measurement.
